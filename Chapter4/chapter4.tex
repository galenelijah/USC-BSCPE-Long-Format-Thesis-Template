This chapter presents the outcomes of the various evaluations conducted throughout the development of the web-based University of San Carlos Patient Information System (USC-PIS). It covers the results of the functional testing (Unit and Integration), non-functional testing (Performance and Security), and User Acceptance Testing (UAT). The findings validate the system’s reliability, efficiency, and usability in modernizing the clinic's healthcare delivery.

\section{Functional Testing Results}

Functional testing was executed to verify that individual components and integrated modules behaved exactly as specified in the system requirements. To ensure a highly reliable and continuous testing environment, a professional Continuous Integration and Continuous Deployment (CI/CD) pipeline was established via GitHub Actions.

An automated testing script (using the Django \texttt{manage.py test} runner) was configured to execute the full Software Quality Assurance (SQA) audit suite upon every code commit to the GitHub repository. This automated DevOps pipeline verified 100\% test coverage across all clinical workflows and security constraints before every production deployment to Heroku. By automating the system auditing process, the researchers ensured that no build failures, syntax errors, or vulnerabilities were introduced into the live clinical system.

The functional testing pipeline was divided into Unit Testing and Integration Testing, ensuring rigorous validation of clinical logic, database encryption constraints, and Role-Based Access Control (RBAC).

\section{User Acceptance Testing (UAT) Results and Feedback}

User Acceptance Testing (UAT) was conducted to gather qualitative and quantitative feedback, assessing whether the platform met the intended objectives of both the clinic staff and the student body.

\subsection{Participant Demographics and Testing Environment}
The User Acceptance Testing (UAT) was conducted using a highly targeted pilot deployment to accurately measure system usability, data synchronization, and operational workflows under real clinic loads. The testing demographic was divided into two distinct user classes: the clinical staff and the student body.

The clinical staff sample consisted of exactly seven (N=7) authorized personnel from the USC Clinic, specifically comprising Doctors, Nurses, and Dentists. This targeted administrative group was tasked with evaluating system efficiency, clinical record creation, and report generation functionalities.

Simultaneously, the student demographic testing was executed targeting 70 out of the 99 enrolled students (approximately 71\%) currently enrolled in the CPE 1202L (Data Structures and Algorithms) course. This yielded a representative student sample size of seventy respondents (N=70). The student participants evaluated the platform's accessibility, mobile responsiveness, the medical certificate issuance pipeline, and the clarity of their personalized health insights.

\subsection{Clinic Staff Evaluation}
Testing was conducted with a clinical sample of N=7 staff members, consisting of two Doctors, three Nurses, and two Dentists. The majority of the respondents (5 out of 7) reported using the system on a daily basis. Based on the statistical means measured on a 5.00-point Likert scale, the staff provided moderate to positive feedback, reflecting both operational improvements and the transitional challenges of adopting a new digital workflow.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{System Efficiency \& Accuracy:} The system achieved a mean score of 3.29/5.00 for efficiency in managing patient records, while data accuracy and the prevention of duplicate records both scored higher at a mean of 3.57/5.00. Staff respondents noted that the platform provides an ``Easy and fast system flow'' and makes ``generating data'' significantly easier. Doctors explicitly praised the organization of patient profiles, stating that the interface makes ``everything a lot more organize[d]''.
    \item \textbf{Reporting \& Workflow Integration:} The generation of reports and the provision of tools for analyzing patient data both received a mean score of 3.29/5.00. During testing, Nurses stated that while the reporting system is efficient, ``adding a feature to filter the patients list strictly by academic year would further improve our ability to generate monitoring reports''. This direct qualitative feedback led to the engineering of the ``Application Retooling'' dynamic filter bar. However, the system's integration with existing manual workflows scored a mean of 3.14/5.00, and the ability to reduce the need for external tools scored 2.71/5.00, indicating that staff still heavily rely on parallel physical documentation.
    \item \textbf{Overall Satisfaction \& Constructive Feedback:} The general satisfaction among clinic staff reached a mean of 3.29/5.00. While many users praised the streamlined data gathering, one Doctor provided highly critical feedback, citing concerns over ``No security features to protect confidential data'' and the continued necessity of ``written records for documentation and possibly, medico-legal purposes''. This critical feedback is particularly valuable; while it reflects a user misunderstanding of the system's invisible backend security, it strongly validates the architectural necessity of the PostgreSQL \texttt{pgcrypto} column-level encryption implemented by the researchers. Furthermore, it highlights a critical need to better educate medical staff on digital security protocols during the training phase.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Student Evaluation}
UAT was conducted with N=70 students from the CPE 1202L course to evaluate the platform’s accessibility, responsiveness, and feature relevance. Based on the survey responses and statistical means (measured on a 5.00-point Likert scale), the system received overwhelmingly positive feedback.

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Accessibility \& Usability:} Students consistently highlighted the system's clean user interface and accessibility. The system scored an exceptional mean of 4.70/5.00 for the ease of logging in and accessing personal records. The clarity of the health information displayed was rated even higher, achieving a mean of 4.76/5.00. Furthermore, system responsiveness and the ability to access the platform anytime and anywhere both achieved a high mean score of 4.61/5.00, indicating a highly stable and available web architecture.
    \item \textbf{Feature Relevance \& Satisfaction:} The student body overwhelmingly identified the platform's core functionalities as highly beneficial, awarding a mean score of 4.60/5.00 for overall feature helpfulness. Specifically, the automated Medical Certificate issuance process received a mean of 4.47/5.00, while the timeliness of system notifications scored 4.49/5.00. In qualitative feedback, users specifically praised the Patient Medical Dashboard and the automated BMI Calculation tools, noting that the ability to obtain a medical certificate without queueing in physical lines was a massive operational improvement.
    \item \textbf{Overall Satisfaction \& Constructive Feedback:} The general satisfaction with the USC-PIS reached an impressive mean of 4.76/5.00. While the system was highly praised, the most recurring constructive suggestions from the demographic were requests for a ``Dark Mode'' toggle and further optimizations for mobile responsiveness to prevent interface stretching. Additionally, several users noted that the strict password creation requirements during registration were slightly confusing and could benefit from clearer prompt instructions. These suggestions have been documented to guide future post-deployment maintenance.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Qualitative Analysis of UAT Feedback}
Beyond the statistical metrics, the qualitative feedback gathered during the User Acceptance Testing provided critical insights into the system's operational impact.

For the clinical staff (N=7), the quantitative results yielded a moderate overall satisfaction mean of 3.29 out of 5.00. While certain personnel, specifically nurses, noted that the system made ``generating data easier,'' the overall metrics for ``ease compared to manual tasks'' (Mean: 2.86) and ``reduction of external tools'' (Mean: 2.71) indicate notable resistance to the digital transition. Qualitative feedback revealed that this friction, including an extreme outlier score of 1.00 from a physician across all questions, was not primarily due to the software's UI, but rather real-world operational constraints. Rather than dismissing this physician's score as an extreme statistical outlier, this feedback must be acknowledged as a highly valid security concern in the medical field. The physician explicitly noted a reluctance to use a personal mobile device for accessing highly sensitive Electronic Medical Records (EMRs) and emphasized the ongoing necessity of physical written records for medico-legal purposes. This highlights a critical finding: while the software utilizes military-grade \texttt{pgcrypto} encryption, it currently acts as a supplement to physical paperwork rather than a legal replacement. Full clinical adoption is heavily bottlenecked not by the software's interface, but by the lack of dedicated, secure university-provided clinic hardware to support these medico-legal realities. In direct response to early feedback regarding monitoring inefficiencies, the researchers executed an ``Application Retooling'' phase, implementing an advanced real-time data filtering architecture that dynamically filters the patients list by year level and academic semester. One clinic physician also emphasized the absolute necessity of documentation security for medico-legal purposes, validating the architectural decision to integrate \texttt{pgcrypto} for encrypted clinical records.

For the student evaluation, the overall satisfaction yielded an impressive mean of 4.76. Students overwhelmingly praised the Patient Medical Dashboard and the Medical Certificate Issuance feature, noting that the ability to obtain a medical certificate without waiting in physical queues was a massive operational improvement. The most recurring constructive feedback from the student body was a request for aesthetic UI optimizations, specifically the addition of a ``Dark Mode'' toggle and enhanced responsiveness to ensure the application fits perfectly on narrower mobile screens.

\section{Database Analysis and Data Quality Assessment}
To validate the reliability of the system under real-world clinical operations, an extensive empirical database analysis was executed targeting the production PostgreSQL 16.8 server. This analysis evaluated data consistency, table normalization, and query latency across the system's 39 normalized tables.

\subsection{Database Consistency and Normalization}
A comprehensive data quality audit demonstrated a robust relationship integrity across all user classes. The structural audit identified that the data schema safely decoupled the authentication credentials (\texttt{authentication\_user}) from the clinical profiles (\texttt{patients\_patient}), resolving over-normalization risks. While the dual-model mapping requires exactly 17 system users to map correctly to respective patient or staff profiles, the audit confirmed that the Foreign Key mapping successfully maintained a 100\% data consistency. Furthermore, stringent system constraints ensure that critical entities, including all medical and dental records, retain strict \texttt{created\_at} and \texttt{updated\_at} immutability, establishing a permanent audit trail required for healthcare compliance.

\subsection{Query Optimization and Latency Benchmarking}
Legacy systems typically experience severe degradation in query speeds (commonly known as the N+1 query problem) when extracting complex relational data. To resolve this, the backend \texttt{PatientViewSet} queries were optimized by prefetching related medical, dental, and demographic records in a single database hit.

\subsection{Cryptographic Benchmarking (pgcrypto)}
To comply with data privacy laws, sensitive plaintext inputs are intercepted by Django \texttt{post\_save} signals and encrypted at the database level. Unit test UT-01 directly audited the PostgreSQL system catalogs and confirmed that sensitive fields, specifically patient names and clinical diagnoses, were successfully converted into advanced symmetric AES-256 binary hashes (\texttt{bytea}) upon rest. Crucially, performance benchmarking (PT-03) verified that executing the \texttt{pgp\_sym\_encrypt} function on clinical records introduced an entirely negligible computational overhead of less than 1.0ms, successfully enforcing military-grade security without compromising system responsiveness.

\subsection{Vulnerability Mitigation and File Security}
The system's File Upload architecture was subjected to rigorous penetration testing (UT-06) to measure its resilience against malicious payload injections. The audit confirmed that the backend validation algorithms correctly verified MIME types and strictly rejected all executable formats. The system achieved a 100\% rejection rate against high-risk scripts (including \texttt{.EXE}, \texttt{.JS}, and \texttt{.PY} files), neutralizing potential system compromises and guaranteeing secure storage of clinical documents.

\subsection{Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) Integrity}
The system’s Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) middleware was stress-tested during Integration Test IT-03. Using simulated cross-role interactions, the audit attempted privilege escalation (e.g., a student account attempting to access doctor-exclusive medical certificate endpoints). The system correctly intercepted all unauthorized requests, returning strictly HTTP 403 (Forbidden) and HTTP 404 (Not Found) responses. Additionally, the SafeList onboarding protocol (UT-05) successfully demonstrated that authorized clinical emails could bypass standard student domain checks and securely receive automatic assignment to professional roles (e.g., DOCTOR or NURSE), proving the system's robust capability to defend endpoints while streamlining administrative deployment.
